1. Project Overview and Scope

Istanbul is the most populous city in Europe. Consequently, in the event of a disaster such as an earthquake, both the scale of potential damage and the demand for assistance would be substantial.

This project focuses on Istanbul, a city widely expected to experience a major earthquake and considerable damage. The objective is not only to analyze buildings and residential areas in terms of structural risk and provide district-level assessments, but also to examine the perceptions of residents living in Istanbul and to compare these findings with the analytical results.

To achieve this, four different datasets were utilized, and a variety of graphs were generated and interpreted in detail to derive meaningful insights.

2.2 General Information About Data

Deprem Senaryosu Analiz Sonuçları is a dataset that contains the results of an earthquake scenario analysis. Its columns mainly include information on the number of buildings by damage level, the number of people by vital status, and pipe damage.

İstanbul Çevresinde Gerçekleşen Depremler 2019-2020 is a dataset on earthquakes that occurred around Istanbul. Each row represents a single earthquake, and the columns provide various details about these events.

2019-2024 Yılları Belediye Nüfusları is a dataset that provides population information for each municipality.

2017 Mahalle Bazlı Bina Sayıları is a dataset about buildings at the neighbourhood level. Each row represents a neighbourhood, and the columns mainly include information about the construction year of buildings and the number of floors.

2.3 Reason of Choice

Istanbul is widely regarded as a city facing a very high earthquake risk, with the potential for severe loss of life and property. Experts frequently warn in the media that a major earthquake, estimated to range between magnitudes 7.2 and 7.6, could strike the city. I chose to focus on this highly relevant issue in order to inform the public through a comprehensive analysis, raise awareness, and encourage precautionary measures that could help minimize the impact of such a disaster.

2.4 Preprocessing

Five datasets were downloaded from the sources listed in Section 2.1 and imported from their original Excel files using readxl. All character columns were converted to UTF-8, repeated whitespace was removed, and empty strings were represented as missing values. District names were standardized in Turkish uppercase and the word “Belediyesi” was removed from the population dataset to create a common ilce_adi key. The historical district name “Eyüp” was also standardized as “Eyüpsultan” to match the more recent datasets.

The earthquake timestamps were converted to UTC date-time values, while magnitude, depth, latitude, and longitude were explicitly checked as numeric variables. Fully duplicated rows were removed, and the district lists were validated across the earthquake scenario, building stock, population, and urban transformation datasets. The cleaned datasets were then stored in istanbul_deprem_verileri.RData.

2.5 District-Level Data Integration

The earthquake scenario and building stock datasets originally contain one row per neighbourhood. Their count variables were summed to produce one row for each district. The population and urban transformation datasets already contain one row per district, so only their variable names and district keys were standardized.

The observed earthquake dataset cannot be assigned directly to Istanbul districts because its coordinates cover a much wider region, including Western Türkiye, the Aegean Sea, the Black Sea, Bulgaria, and surrounding areas. Assigning these events to Istanbul districts would therefore be misleading. Instead, this fifth dataset contributes regional context variables, including the total number of observed earthquakes, the number recorded within 100 km of central Istanbul, mean and maximum magnitude, median depth, and the observation period. These contextual values are identical for all district rows and must not be interpreted as district-specific earthquake counts.

The resulting ilce_birlesik table contains exactly one row for each of Istanbul’s 39 districts. It combines projected earthquake consequences, building characteristics, annual population, urban transformation opinions, and regional observed-earthquake context. It also includes derived variables for the proportion of pre-1980 buildings, the proportion reporting a need for urban transformation, and population change between 2019 and 2024.

find_iqr_outliers <- function(data, variable) {
  values <- data[[variable]]
  q1 <- quantile(values, 0.25, na.rm = TRUE)
  q3 <- quantile(values, 0.75, na.rm = TRUE)
  iqr_value <- q3 - q1

  data |>
    mutate(outlier_value = .data[[variable]]) |>
    filter(
      outlier_value < q1 - 1.5 * iqr_value |
        outlier_value > q3 + 1.5 * iqr_value

}

3.2 Normalized District Risk Score

3.2.1 Method

A composite score was developed to compare the relative earthquake risk of Istanbul’s districts. The score does not estimate the probability that an earthquake will occur. Instead, it combines the projected consequences, structural vulnerability, infrastructure and shelter pressure, and the scale of exposed population and buildings under the scenario data.

Each indicator was normalized to the 0–100 range using min–max normalization:

\[ z_{ij} = 100 \times \frac{x_{ij} - \min(x_j)} {\max(x_j) - \min(x_j)} \]

where \(x_{ij}\) is the value of district \(i\) for indicator \(j\). A value of 0 represents the lowest observed value among the 39 districts and 100 represents the highest. Indicators within each dimension were averaged, and the four dimension scores were assigned equal weights. Equal weights were chosen because no expert-derived weighting scheme was available.

min_max_100 <- function(x) {
  limits <- range(x, na.rm = TRUE)

  if (limits[1] == limits[2]) {
    return(rep(0, length(x)))
  }

  100 * (x - limits[1]) / (limits[2] - limits[1])
}

3.5 Results

The equal-weight composite score ranks Adalar, Fatih, Bakırköy, Küçükçekmece, and Bahçelievler as the five highest-risk districts. These districts reach high scores for different reasons. Adalar has the highest per-capita human-consequence and infrastructure scores despite having the lowest exposure score, while Fatih combines high human consequences with substantial structural vulnerability. Küçükçekmece and Bahçelievler have lower structural scores than Fatih but much larger exposed populations and building stocks.

Adalar’s first-place ranking requires particular care in interpretation. Its small population increases per-capita rates, so its score reflects high modeled consequences relative to population rather than the largest absolute number of affected people. For this reason, the component scores and raw counts should always be considered alongside the overall ranking.

The alternative weighting scheme produces a Spearman rank correlation of 0.965 with the equal-weight ranking, and nine of the ten highest-ranked districts remain in the top ten. This indicates that the broad ranking is relatively stable, although some individual districts change position when greater weight is assigned to human and structural consequences.

4. Results and Key Takeaways

Istanbul is the most populous city in Europe, which means that in the event of a disaster such as an earthquake, both the scale of destruction and the demand for assistance would be extremely high.

The aim of this project is to examine structural vulnerability, make district-based assessments, and evaluate the views of people living in Istanbul.

Five datasets were used in this study, and a series of plots were produced and interpreted in detail.

This study identified the districts that are most likely to experience building and infrastructure damage in the event of an earthquake. In this way, it provides guidance on where aid should be directed, how it should be delivered, and to what extent support may be required.

Finally, this study serves both as a guide and as a means of encouraging society to be better prepared for such a disaster.

More specifically, the study has:

identified the areas most prone to earthquake risk and the districts most vulnerable, visualized these findings on maps, examined the condition of buildings and infrastructure in Istanbul.
